## Design Context

### Users
DayBridge is built for older adults who live independently and for the small care circles around them: adult children, neighbors, aides, volunteers, and friends. The primary user may be using the app on a phone or tablet in the kitchen, living room, clinic waiting room, or right before leaving the house.

Their day often has more handoffs than a typical student or developer day: pills taken as prescribed, rides, paperwork, meals, phone calls, appointments, small home tasks, and moments when asking for help feels emotionally costly.

The core job is to turn "I hope I remember everything today" into "I can see what matters, mark what happened, and ask for help without a long explanation."

### Brand Personality
DayBridge should feel calm, respectful, practical, and independence-preserving.

The interface should never feel like a childish senior app or a medical device that talks down to people. It should feel like a sturdy daily board on a kitchen table: readable, direct, reliable, and warm without being sentimental.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use a daylight, high-contrast visual system with large controls and plain labels. Favor natural greens, clear ink, and warm amber attention states. Avoid productivity-app jargon, tiny text, flashy motion, dark sci-fi styling, and decorative complexity.

Reference direction:
- Public library signage for clarity and dignity
- Medication packaging hierarchy for readable urgency
- Calendar and kitchen whiteboard ergonomics for routine visibility

Anti-direction:
- No galaxy / mission-control metaphor
- No student language
- No gamified XP-first framing
- No medical-advice posture
- No tiny controls or dense command palettes
- No purple-blue AI SaaS gradients

### Design Principles
1. Make the day scannable in five seconds.
2. Put independence before surveillance: caregivers support, but the senior owns the day.
3. Use plain, literal language for actions and status.
4. Keep safety boundaries visible: reminders and coordination, not diagnosis or dosage advice.
5. Design for lower dexterity and lower vision with generous hit targets, strong contrast, and fewer choices per screen.
